An interactive model showing how unpredictability drives wait times in Ontario's kidney transplant system — and what pooling can do about it.

The situation

In Ontario, patients with End-Stage Renal Disease (ESRD) require a kidney transplant to survive long-term. While waiting, they are kept alive by dialysis — a gruelling treatment with only a 45% five-year survival rate. Two transplant programs serve the province: one at London Health Sciences Centre (LHSC) and one in Toronto. They operate as completely separate wait-lists, each constrained by a single bottleneck: the supply of donor organs.

In queuing theory, performance degrades exponentially as a system approaches 100% capacity. Toronto is at 99.92% — effectively at its limit. Even a single additional patient per year is enough to collapse the system entirely. This simulator uses Kingman's G/G/1 approximation to show how two independent forces — arrival variability and service variability — interact with that near-saturation to determine how long patients wait.

Two levers that control wait times
Arrival variability (cₐ) — how random are referrals?
When cₐ = 0, patients arrive at perfectly regular intervals. When cₐ = 1 (Poisson), arrivals are random and memoryless — the clinical baseline. When cₐ = 2, referrals come in unpredictable bursts. This is controllable through standardised ESRD screening protocols.
Service variability (cₛ) — how random is organ supply?
Reflects the unpredictability of deceased donor availability. At cₛ = 1.0 (exponential), this matches standard M/M/1. At cₛ = 1.5, organ supply has a heavier tail — long dry spells followed by clusters. Reducible through better donor identification and organ preservation logistics.

Why variability amplifies waiting — the Kingman formula
Wait ≈ [capacity pressure] × [variability]
Lq ≈ ρ² / 2(1−ρ)  ×  (cₐ² + cₛ²)   |   W = Lq/λ + 1/μ

The left bracket is the capacity amplifier — fixed by arrival and service rates. The right bracket is the variability multiplier — controlled by how unpredictable things are. They multiply together. Toronto's amplifier is 625×. Every unit of chaos gets magnified 625 times.

What the numbers mean in practice

Reducing variability requires no new organs and no capital investment — only operational discipline. The simulator above quantifies what each intervention is worth.

Reducing arrival chaos (cₐ: 1.0 → 0.5)
Achievable through standardised ESRD screening pathways across referring hospitals. Estimated impact: ~20–30% reduction in wait times at no additional cost in donor supply.
Reducing service chaos (cₛ: 1.5 → 1.0)
Achievable through better organ procurement logistics, improved preservation protocols, and predictive analytics for donor identification. Estimated impact: ~38% reduction in wait times.
📌 The amplifier values above are fixed by the system's utilisation rate (ρ) — they don't change with the sliders. What changes is the variability multiplied by that amplifier. Toronto's 625× amplifier means every improvement to predictability yields outsized returns.
